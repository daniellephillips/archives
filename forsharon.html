Charles and Sharon Schlosser Story

Charles Schlosser stood at the edge of the Columbia River, the gentle waves lapping at the shore. The river was a cruel reminder of what had been lost, of the life snatched away in its depths. His wife, Sharon, had been found here, her body floating and lifeless, on an autumn day in 1992.

Sharon Frances Schlosser, co-owner of the C&S Nursery, was last seen alive on the morning of October 20th. She had left their Clackamas home, supposedly headed for a business meeting in Mount Vernon, Washington. But she never arrived. Six days later, a passing tugboat crew discovered her body near Hayden Island, bearing the grim marks of a brutal homicide.

From the very beginning, the investigation was mired in suspicion and confusion. Charles, the bereaved husband, was the first to report her missing, claiming she had vanished. Detectives noted his reluctance to cooperate fully, and his lawyer's constant presence didn't help assuage their suspicions.

Charles's sons, Christopher, Timothy, Tony, and Charles Jr., found themselves under the harsh glare of the investigation. They invoked their Fifth Amendment rights, refusing to testify before the grand jury. Their silence only deepened the cloud of doubt hanging over the Schlosser family.

Sharon's life had seemed idyllic to outsiders. She was an active community member and was involved in numerous clubs and associations. Her marriage to Charles had appeared solid, yet there were whispers of financial strain and underlying tensions. Some suggested that Sharon had discovered something unsavory about Charles and his sons, something that might have cost her life.

The investigation dragged on, year after year, with no charges filed. The family's boat, a 47-foot Chris-Craft named "Nikki," was reported stolen and later found burnt, a stark symbol of the tangled web of deceit and tragedy. The case became one of the area's most vexing unsolved murders, a haunting specter for the detectives who had worked tirelessly but fruitlessly to bring Sharon's killer to justice.

In a desperate bid for resolution, Sharon's children filed a wrongful death suit against Charles. The courtroom battles were fierce, with testimonies that painted Charles in an increasingly sinister light. Witnesses claimed he had tried to hire someone to kill Sharon, offering a Corvette and cash for the deed. Yet, the jury remained unconvinced, their verdict deadlocked, leaving the case unresolved.

Despite the civil court's inability to deliver a decisive outcome, the fight for justice persisted. Sharon's mother, Agnes Mahoney, never gave up hope. Each year, on Sharon's birthday, she placed a small ad in the obituary section of the newspaper, a poignant plea for justice and a reminder that Sharon's murder remained unsolved.

As the years passed, the river kept its secrets, its silent waters a witness to the violence and betrayal that had shattered a family. The image of Sharon's body drifting in the current was a haunting memory for those who had loved her. The questions remained unanswered and unyielding: Who had killed Sharon Schlosser, and why?

For Charles, the shadow of suspicion never lifted. He remained in the community, a man haunted by the allegations and the loss of his wife. Whether guilty or innocent, his life was irrevocably marked by the events of that fateful October. And so, the Columbia River continued to flow, a silent keeper of the mysteries that swirled beneath its surface.
